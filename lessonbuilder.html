Concept 1 of 4

Ohm's Law: The Core Relationship

Ohm’s Law establishes the fundamental triad of electrical circuit analysis: Voltage ($V$), Current ($I$), and Resistance ($R$). Voltage acts as the electrical pressure pushing charges through a circuit, Current represents the structural rate of flow of those charges past a point per second, and Resistance signifies the physical opposition to that flow. Mathematically balanced, the formula states that the voltage across a conductor is directly proportional to the current flowing through it, yielding:

$$V = I \times R$$

Where: V = Volts (V), I = Amperes (A), and R = Ohms ($\Omega$)

Concrete Example: Consider a standard automotive 12V battery connected to a headlamp assembly that possesses an internal electrical resistance of 3 $\Omega$. To determine how much current is pulled from the battery, we rearrange Ohm's Law to calculate $I = V / R$. Substituting our values gives $12\text{ V} / 3\text{ }\Omega = 4\text{ A}$.

Concept 2 of 4

Electrical Power: Joule's Law

While Ohm's law calculates physical movement limits, Electrical Power ($P$) measures the rate at which energy is delivered or transformed by a circuit element per unit of time. Joule’s Law describes how power is directly dependent on both the delivery mechanism pressure (Voltage) and the volume of charge carriers acting within it (Current). By substituting Ohm's variables into Joule's standard formula, we can also derive alternative versions linked directly to circuit friction (Resistance), yielding multiple tools to measure active work or thermal dispersion.

$$P = V \times I = I^2 \times R = \frac{V^2}{R}$$

Where: P = Watts (W), V = Volts (V), I = Amps (A), and R = Ohms ($\Omega$)

Concrete Example: An industrial space heater with an elements resistance rating of 10 $\Omega$ is wired directly into a standard 120V electrical outlet. To calculate how much thermal power output it generates without calculating current first, we deploy the final variant of the formula: $P = V^2 / R$. This produces $(120\text{ V})^2 / 10\text{ }\Omega = 14,400 / 10 = 1,440\text{ W}$ (or 1.44 kW).

Concept 3 of 4

Series Equivalent Resistance

In engineering architecture, components are connected together in distinct structural patterns. A **Series Circuit** features components arranged sequentially along a single continuous pathway. Because there is only one route for electric charges to travel, the total resistance of the entire layout accumulates directly. Adding more resistors in series increases opposition to current flow, acting exactly as if you were making a singular resistive channel longer.

$$R_{eq} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3 + \dots + R_n$$

Where: $R_{eq}$ is the total net equivalent resistance in ohms

Concrete Example: If three signaling LED indicators with sequential internal line resistances of 150 $\Omega$, 200 $\Omega$, and 350 $\Omega$ are wired directly end-to-end in a strict single-loop series framework, their total unified layout resistance becomes a basic summation: $R_{eq} = 150 + 200 + 350 = 700\text{ }\Omega$.

Concept 4 of 4

Parallel Equivalent Resistance

When circuit paths branch away from each other, they create a **Parallel Circuit**. In this framework, all component inputs share a common node connection, and all outputs share another. Crucially, providing alternate routing pathways makes it *easier* overall for electric charges to flow. Consequently, the total equivalent resistance ($R_{eq}$) of parallel systems is always **smaller** than the smallest individual resistor value. It is modeled using reciprocal mathematics:

$$\frac{1}{R_{eq}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3} + \dots + \frac{1}{R_n}$$

Tip for two resistors: $R_{eq} = (R_1 \times R_2) / (R_1 + R_2)$

Concrete Example: Imagine wire leads splitting to supply power concurrently to two audio speakers arranged side-by-side in parallel. If Speaker A measures 8 $\Omega$ and Speaker B measures 8 $\Omega$, we utilize the parallel reciprocal formula: $1/R_{eq} = 1/8 + 1/8 = 2/8$. Flipping this fraction to find $R_{eq}$ gives $8 / 2 = 4\text{ }\Omega$. Notice the aggregate structural resistance was cut completely in half by opening that parallel lane option.
