Why does a Systems Analyst care about Cash?

SAD 101 Lecture with Professor Douglas

"Hello, class! Welcome to college-level Systems Analysis and Design. Often, beginners think our job is just drawing code diagrams and building databases. But here is the real-world truth:"

"In the corporate world, every technology upgrade is an investment. If you propose building a $100,000 custom software solution, your boss's first question will not be 'What code language does it run?' It will be 'How much money does this save or make us?'"

We call this Cost-Benefit Analysis. In this quick interactive lab, we are going to learn how to prove our projects are financially smart using two essential tools:

1. Return on Investment (ROI)

Measures the raw efficiency of your investment. It answers: "How much profit did we make relative to our costs?"

2. Net Present Value (NPV)

Considers the Time Value of Money. It answers: "Is our multi-year project worth the wait when future cash is worth less?"

💵 Lesson 1: Return on Investment (ROI)

Let's calculate simple financial yields.

ROI is the absolute easiest way to evaluate a project. Let's look at the standard academic formula:

$$ROI = \frac{\text{Total Benefits} - \text{Total Costs}}{\text{Total Costs}} \times 100\%$$

💡 A systems example: You buy a new cloud inventory database software for $10,000. It reduces processing time and saves you $15,000 in labor costs. Your ROI is 50%.

⏳ Lesson 2: The Time Value of Money

Why future money is worth less than a dollar today.

Imagine a customer offers to pay you $1,000 today, OR $1,000 in three years. Which do you choose?

You choose today! Why? Because if you have the $1,000 today, you can invest it (for example, in a high-yield savings account earning interest) or use it to grow your business. By year three, that $1,000 would have grown to much more.

To compare future savings of an IT project against costs incurred today, we must discount future money back to its equivalent value today. We use this formula:

$$PV = \frac{FV}{(1 + r)^n}$$

Where $FV$ is Future Value, $r$ is the Discount Rate (interest rate), and $n$ is the number of years.

SAD Cheat Sheet
🚨 The Hurdle Rule:

If $NPV \ge 0$, you build it. If $NPV < 0$, you reject the project.

📉 Discount Shrinkage:

The higher the discount rate (hurdle), the harder it is for long-term projects to look profitable today.
